Maker vs taker, in plain words

Polymarket only charges the taker. That's the whole thing.
Taker: you hit an order that's already sitting on the book. Instant fill. You pay the fee.
Maker: you post your own limit order and wait. Someone else hits you. You pay nothing.
Same trade. Same price. Opposite fee outcome.
When you click "buy" and take the current price, you are the taker, and you pay for the convenience of being instant.
Immagine
When you place a limit order that rests on the book and gets filled later, you added liquidity instead of removing it so the platform charges you zero.
That is why limit orders = 0 fees.
It's not a loophole. It's the design. 
Makers feed the order book, so the book pays them instead of charging them.
The fee is worst exactly where arb lives

Here's the part that should change how you build.
The taker fee is not flat. It follows a curve:
fee = contracts x rate x p x (1 - p)
That p x (1 - p) term peaks when the price is 50 cents.
At 50/50, the fee is at its absolute maximum. 
At the extremes (5 cents, 95 cents), it shrinks toward nothing.

You pay zero fee on the trade.

Qué es realmente Polymarket y de dónde sale el dinero

Para entender por qué funciona esta estrategia, primero debes comprender la mecánica de Polymarket. Es un mercado de predicción: una plataforma donde los usuarios operan sobre el resultado de eventos del mundo real. La estructura es simple. Para cualquier evento dado — ¿estará Bitcoin más alto o más bajo en 15 minutos?, ¿subirá las tasas la Fed este mes?, ¿ganará el candidato X las elecciones? — los usuarios pueden comprar acciones del "Sí" o del "No".
Cada acción cotiza entre $0 y $1. El precio refleja la estimación colectiva del mercado sobre la probabilidad. Un contrato que cotiza a $0.73 implica que el mercado cree que hay un 73% de probabilidades de que el resultado sea "Sí". Si aciertas, tu acción se liquida a $1.00. Si fallas, se liquida a $0.00. El mecanismo es elegante, la matemática es directa y los mercados son líquidos: el volumen de negociación semanal de Polymarket superó los $2 mil millones a principios de 2026.
La categoría clave de la plataforma para el trading automatizado son sus contratos cripto de corta duración: preguntas de subida/bajada de 5 y 15 minutos sobre Bitcoin y Ethereum. Cada pocos minutos, se abre un nuevo contrato preguntando si el BTC estará más alto o más bajo al vencimiento. Estos contratos se resuelven rápido, ofrecen retroalimentación inmediata y, lo más importante, presentan una vulnerabilidad estructural que los bots pueden explotar.
Polymarket actualiza los precios de sus contratos con mayor lentitud de lo que se mueve el activo subyacente en los exchanges centralizados. Siempre hay un retraso (lag) entre el momento en que ocurre un cambio de precio en Binance o Coinbase y el momento en que ese cambio se refleja en las cuotas de los contratos de Polymarket. En 2024, ese retraso promediaba unos 12 segundos. Para el primer trimestre de 2026, la competencia lo había reducido a aproximadamente 2.7 segundos. ¿Por qué existe este retraso en Polymarket?

Es una pregunta razonable. Si la ineficiencia es tan obvia, ¿por qué Polymarket no la ha solucionado? La respuesta es estructural. Polymarket es un mercado de predicción descentralizado: utiliza un modelo de CLOB (Libro de Órdenes de Límite Central) donde los precios se mueven solo cuando se colocan y casan órdenes. A diferencia de un exchange centralizado con una mesa de market-making dedicada, los precios de Polymarket solo cambian cuando los traders publican órdenes activamente.
En la práctica, esto significa que en los segundos posteriores a un movimiento importante en Binance, el lado "inteligente" del contrato de Polymarket —el lado que se beneficiará del movimiento— tiene pocos vendedores dispuestos a operar con cuotas obsoletas. El mercado es temporalmente unilateral. Los bots que reconocen este desequilibrio y lo llenan comprando el lado "obvio" están proporcionando liquidez a cambio de tomar el otro lado. Son recompensados por ser rápidos y por acertar en la dirección del retraso.
Polymarket ha tomado medidas para acelerar la actualización de precios. El retraso ha bajado de más de 12 segundos en 2024 a unos 2,7 segundos a principios de 2026. Pero cerrar la brecha por completo requeriría creadores de mercado automatizados en tiempo real que estuvieran altamente capitalizados y dirigidos por algoritmos, que es esencialmente lo que ya son los bots de arbitraje. Arbitraje de Oráculo (Tasa de acierto: 78–85%)Una oportunidad más robusta pero menos frecuente. Redes de oráculos como Chainlink publican precios on-chain que Polymarket usa para la liquidación. Cuando el feed de Chainlink muestra un precio y el contrato de Polymarket implica otro, la dirección de la liquidación se conoce con casi total certeza.
Trading basado en Noticias (Tasa de acierto: 60–75%)Aquí es donde la capacidad de razonamiento de Claude es más relevante. La IA puede actualizar la probabilidad implícita antes de que los humanos terminen de leer el titular.
Market Making (Retorno: 2–5% mensual)La estrategia más consistente y difícil de arruinar. El bot coloca órdenes de compra y venta simultáneas en ambos lados, capturando el spread (diferencial) entre el precio de compra y venta sin tomar una dirección. El riesgo es el inventario: si el mercado se mueve bruscamente, puedes quedarte atrapado con contratos perdedores.

El modo de fallo más peligroso no es una mala estrategia. GUIDA NUMERO 3
Your arb bot reads the last tick. 
Your scanner sees YES at 0.48, NO at 0.49, sum 0.97, and screams arb.
It places the trade, but the gap was never there.
Or worse. A real 4-cent gap sits open and your bot misses it entirely.
Both come from the same mistake: trusting a single tick.
The paper that measured $40M in real Polymarket arb didn't look at prices the way your bot does: arxiv.org/pdf/2508.03474
It used VWAP. 

Here is the exact method, and why it's the difference between catching gaps and chasing ghosts.
Why a single tick lies

The last traded price is one trade.
Maybe it was 5 shares. 
Maybe it was a fat finger. 
Maybe it printed an hour ago and nothing has traded since.
Your bot treats that one number as "the price."
Immagine
So when you sum YES + NO off the last tick, you get a number built on two random, possibly stale, possibly tiny trades.
Sometimes that sum lands at 0.97 and looks like free money, but the real book is sitting at 1.00 and the gap is a mirage.
Sometimes a true gap is open and your stale ticks hide it.
Either way you're making a real-money decision off noise.
One tick is not the price.
It's the last thing that happened to print.
Volume-weighting in plain words

VWAP fixes this. 
Volume-weighted average price.
Instead of "what was the last trade," it asks "what price did the market actually transact at, weighted by size."
Immagine
A 1,000-share fill at 0.42 should count far more than a 5-share fill at 0.40.
Last-tick treats them the same, VWAP doesn't.
Here's the whole computation. 
No plumbing, just the math:
python
# tick = (price, token_amount) for each executed bid in your window
def vwap(ticks):
    notional = sum(price * size for price, size in ticks)
    volume   = sum(size for _, size in ticks)
    if volume == 0:
        return None          # nothing traded - no real price
    return notional / volume
That's it. 
Sum of price times size, divided by total size.
Now you have the price the market actually paid, not the last print that happened to land.
The window

You don't VWAP all of history. 
You VWAP a slice of time.
This is the part people get wrong.
The paper averaged over a single block, then carried the last known price forward for up to about 2.5 hours if a leg went quiet.

And set it to zero if the token truly stopped trading, because a dead token has no value.
Why so tight?
Because arb lives in volatility.
Make your window too wide and you smear across the exact moment the gap opened, averaging it out of existence.
Make it too narrow and you're back to noise.
Immagine
A short rolling window with sensible carry-forward is the balance. You want the price as it is right now, built from real recent fills, not a smooth average of the whole day.
Tune the window length. It's the single biggest knob on this whole thing.
The flag

Now you put the legs together and check the gap.
Sum the VWAP of every position. 
In a clean market they should sum to a dollar. When they don't, that's your edge.
python
def arb_flag(legs, threshold=0.02):
    vwaps = [vwap(t) for t in legs]   # one tick-list per outcome

    # any leg with no trades this window - skip, not enough info
    if any(v is None for v in vwaps):
        return None

    # a leg already above 0.95 means the market is basically resolved
    # that's settlement, not arb - skip it
    if any(v > 0.95 for v in vwaps):
        return None

    gap = abs(1 - sum(vwaps))
    if gap > threshold:
        side = "long" if sum(vwaps) < 1 else "short"
        return {"gap": round(gap, 4), "side": side}
    return None
The 0.02 is the paper's detection line - flag when the VWAP sum drifts more than 2 cents off a dollar.
The 0.95 skip matters as much as the flag. If a leg is already near a dollar, the outcome is decided. 
Immagine
There's no real mispricing left, just the market settling. Trading that isn't arb, it's noise dressed as a gap.
The catch

Two things, or this turns on you.
First, VWAP from executed bids underestimates the real margin. 
The paper says so directly because you're averaging trades that already happened, the true gap available right now is usually bigger than what your VWAP shows.
So treat the flagged gap as a floor, not a ceiling.
Second, a 2-cent flag is not a 2-cent trade.
Two cents is enough to notice. It is not enough to act.
The paper only bothered with opportunities worth at least 5 cents on the dollar, because below that the fees and the non-atomic fill risk eat you alive.
So: flag at 2 cents, trade at 5-plus, and only as a maker so the fee doesn't take the rest.
Detect wide and act narrow.
Final thought

Your bot is probably making decisions off the last print right now.
The last print is one trade. It lies.
VWAP is the price the market actually transacted at, and it's the same lens the wallets pulling real arb out of Polymarket look through.
Weight by volume.
Window it tight.
Flag at two cents, trade at five, capture as a maker.
